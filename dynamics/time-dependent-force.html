Time-Dependent Force

< Return

We ended off our tour of kinematics with circular motion and variable acceleration, or what we described as jerk. Now, we end our tour of dynamics with revisiting circular motion and variable force.

Let's analyze the Law of Acceleration again, but instead we will let acceleration be a function of time; in other words, acceleration will vary over time. With this, force now depends on time, as well.

F(t)=ma(t)

This is nice and all, but usually we want to get the position of an object over time. Thus, we rearrange to get the acceleration.

F(t)m=a(t)

This is promising. Recall from our study of kinematics that acceleration is the derivative of velocity, so we substitute that in for the acceleration function.

F(t)m=ddtv
F(t)mdt=dv

We now integrate both sides. Since we generally want the velocity at any time t, we integrate from t=0 to our chosen time. The right side also has the same treatment, going from the initial velocity to the velocity at the time we want.

∫0tF(t)mdt=∫v0vdv
∫0tF(t)mdt=∫v0vdv
∫0tF(t)mdt=v−v0

Here, we will make the assumption that the mass is constant. Of course, in more complicated systems, it isn't, but here it is a good enough approximation.

∫0tF(t)⋅1mdt=v−v0
1m∫0tF(t)dt=v−v0
1m∫0tF(t)dt+v0=v(t)

We can integrate this again to get the position, however we will leave that for an exercise below.

Exercise 1

(HRK E5.48c) The position of a particle of mass 2.17 kg traveling in a straight line is given by the following equation.
x=(0.179m/s⁴)t4−(2.08m/s²)t2+(17.1m)
Find the force on the particle at time t=7.18s.

Solution

We will first differentiate the position function to get the velocity and acceleration function. We will first drop all the units, but keep them in mind for later.

v=ddt(0.179t4−2.08t2+17.1)
v=0.716t3−4.16t

Again, we differentiate to get acceleration.

a=ddt(0.716t3−4.16t)
a=2.148t2−4.16

Now, we can substitute the time value here and multiply it with the mass, but we'll just get the force equation instead. Noting the Law of Acceleration, we have this.

F(t)=ma(t)
F(t)=(2.17)(2.148t2−4.16)
F(t)=4.661t2−9.027

As we substitute, we get that the force is 231N.

You may be surprised on the equations below. Here, the power rule may not be as useful. Instead, you may have to use other laws for integrals. You may also need to consult a table of integrals.

Exercise 2a

(HRK E5.49a) A particle is subjected to a net force given by F=F0(1−tT).

Show that at the instant the force vanishes at t=T, the speed of the particle is given by v=v0+a0T2, where T, v0, F0, and m are constants where a0=F0m.

Solution

We will use the equation we made above.

1m∫0tF(t)dt+v0=v(t)

We first substitute in the function for the force, and take out all the constants. We also note that a0=F0m, so we substitute that in.

1m∫0tF0(1−tT)dt+v0=v(t)
a0∫0t(1−tT)dt+v0=v(t)
a0(∫0t1dt−∫0ttTdt)+v0=v(t)
a0(∫0tdt−1T∫0ttdt)+v0=v(t)

We now evaluate the two integrals.

a0(t|0t−1Tt22|0t)+v0=v(t)
a0([t−0]−1T[t22−0])+v0=v(t)
a0(t−t22T)+v0=v(t)

Now, we set t=T.

a0(T−T22T)+v0=v(T)
a0(T−T2)+v0=v(T)
a0T2+v0=v(T)

This gives us the required equation.

Exercise 2b

(HRK E5.49b) Let the initial position of the particle be x0=0. Thus, show that at t=T, the position of the particle is given by x=v0T+a0T23.

Solution

We will differentiate the general equation we got for the velocity.

∫v(t)dt=x(t)
∫(a0(t−t22T)+v0)dt=x(t)
∫a0(t−t22T)dt+∫v0dt=x(t)
a0(∫(t−t22T)dt)+∫v0dt=x(t)
a0(∫tdt−∫t22Tdt)+∫v0dt=x(t)

We now integrate.

a0(t22−t36T)+v0t=x(t)

Now, we set t=T.

a0(T22−T36T)+v0T=x(T)
a0(3T26−T26)+v0T=x(T)
a0(2T26)+v0T=x(T)
a0T23+v0T=x(T)

This gives us the required equation.

Exercise 3

(HRK P5.20a) A force given by F=F0e−tT is applied to a particle of mass m initially at rest. When t=T, the force is removed. At this instance, what is the speed of the particle in terms of the constants F0, T, and m?

Solution

We will use the equation we concocted above.

1m∫0tF(t)dt+v0=v(t)

Let's first substitute the force in. We also drop the initial velocity, seeing as it is at rest. Then, we take out the constant F0.

1m∫0tF0e−tTdt=v(t)
F0m∫0te−tTdt=v(t)

To solve the integral, we will use u-substitution. Here, setting u=−tT.

u=−tT
du=−dtT
−Tdu=dt

Substituting back in, we have this. We also take out the constants brought by our u-substitution.

F0m∫0teu(−Tdu)=v(t)
−TF0m∫0teudu=v(t)

Finally, we can evaluate this integral.

−TF0meu|0t=v(t)
−TF0me−tT|0t=v(t)
−TF0m[e−tT−e0]=v(t)
−TF0m[e−tT−1]=v(t)

Now, we set t=T.

−TF0m[e−1−1]=v(T)
−TF0m[1e−1]=v(T)
TF0m[1−1e]=v(T)

This is the equation for the velocity given the time when the force disappears.
